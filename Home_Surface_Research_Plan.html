Home Surface Research Plan
Concept Gauntlet · Shadow Inbox · Experience Prototypes
Draft for Discussion · March 2026 · UX Research

Topline Summary

We're researching the Home Surface — a new primary email screen that replaces the chronological inbox with an intelligent, life-organized experience powered by the Life Graph. This plan answers two questions in parallel:

  1. Does the experience work? We start with 4 distinct experience visions, rapidly evaluate them through a Concept Gauntlet (landing pages + video walkthroughs via dScout Express), then build the 2–3 winners as full interactive, instrumented web prototypes tested by an external panel.
  2. Is the intelligence ready? We run a 1–2 week Shadow Inbox simulation with 5 internal dogfooders — comparing what the system would have shown against their real inbox. This calibrates accuracy, surfaces failure modes, and produces a clear recommendation on whether we can build the intelligence in-house or need vendor consultation.

Target archetype: The overwhelmed millennial parent — permission slips mixed with mortgage alerts mixed with soccer carpool. If this works for them, it works for everyone.

Timeline: 5 weeks. Gauntlet runs Weeks 1–2. Intelligence calibration runs Weeks 2–3. Experience prototypes tested Weeks 3–5.

Week 5 deliverables:

  1. Experience direction deck — the 2 strongest directions with data, for cross-functional leadership
  2. Intelligence readiness assessment — can we build this, or do we need outside help?

Participants: 5 internal dogfooders (Weeks 1–3, then roll off) + 5–8 external dScout recruits (Weeks 3–5, fresh eyes on prototypes).

Full Research Plan

1. Strategic Context

What we're building

Yahoo Mail Premium (~$20/mo) — a subscription built on AI-powered features that make the inbox understand the user's life. Core technology: the Life Graph, a frontier LLM-powered model of the user's life built from email content and behavioral signals.

What we're researching

The Home Surface — a new primary email screen replacing the chronological list view. It must handle:

  • Incoming: New emails, triaged and prioritized by life context
  • Upcoming: Obligations, deadlines, and events extracted from email history
  • The middle space: Lifting select incoming items into upcoming when they contain future obligations

Target archetype

The overwhelmed millennial parent — someone whose email system has gotten away from them. Permission slips mixed with mortgage alerts mixed with soccer carpool. If the Home Surface works for this person, it works for everyone.

Core thesis requirements

  • Low cognitive lift
  • Radically improved signal-to-noise
  • Leverages the intelligent context graph (Life Graph)
  • Designed for rapid processing
  • Creates a sense of low-lift completion
  • Diverges from the sender/subject/snippet chronological inbox
  • One surface that intuitively flexes (not a linked network of surfaces)
  • Comprehensive enough to replace the inbox, not sit alongside it

Established principles (validated — do NOT re-test)

Prior research from the Google Sprint, hackathon prototype, and ongoing concept work established these with sufficient conviction:

  1. Cognitive chunking works. Bite-sized chunks provide emotional and cognitive relief.
  2. Whole-picture upshot is non-negotiable. "Since you were last here" is required to avoid FOMO. Without it, any new surface feels like it's hiding something.
  3. Privacy is secondary to value. Users accept Life Graph privacy implications if the intelligence delivers genuine value.
  4. Upcoming ≥ incoming. Tracking upcoming obligations is at least as important as triaging new messages.
  5. Replacement, not addition. Must become the primary experience, not "yet another surface."

2. Open Research Questions

The Home Surface design space is multi-dimensional. These are the questions this research is designed to answer:

Information Architecture

How is the surface organized? What are the primary structural units — categories, priority tiers, temporal groupings? Where does incoming end and upcoming begin — visible boundary, gradient, or fluid integration? How does "since you were last here" relate spatially to the rest?

Range: Distinct sections ↔ fully integrated by relevance ↔ category-first with mixed incoming/upcoming

Content Density

How much does the surface show at once? How much context per item — does the micro-brief replace or augment sender/subject/snippet? How deep does each grouping go before requiring drill-down? How does density shift between light and heavy days?

Range: Headline-only ↔ full micro-briefs inline ↔ category summaries with counts

Interaction Patterns & Triage Mechanics

What's the interaction grammar? Does the system decide, does the user confirm, or is it a spectrum? What are the gestures for surface action vs. drill-in? How does "lifting" an incoming item into upcoming feel?

Range: Per-item approve/defer/dismiss ↔ batch by category ↔ system pre-triaged, user reviews

Behavior Patterns

How do people actually move through this surface? What's the scan pattern — top to bottom, by category, by urgency? What triggers a drill-down vs. acting from the surface? How does a quick check-in differ from a dedicated session?

Captured via: Lab observation, scroll/tap instrumentation, Express self-report

Cognitive Framing & Mental Model

What mental model do users adopt? Briefing, dashboard, to-do list, assistant, or something new? Does the framing create calm or anxiety?

Range: Briefing you read ↔ dashboard you monitor ↔ assistant that reports ↔ canvas you scan

Completion Model

What replaces the unread count as the "I'm done" signal? Is it an explicit state, a progress signal, or the absence of urgency? Does the feeling reset daily or accumulate?

Range: Explicit state ↔ progressive depletion ↔ ambient calm ↔ summary receipt

Temporal Dynamics

How does the surface change throughout the day? Is morning different from evening? After triage, does the surface contract, rearrange, or celebrate? What about returning after hours away vs. minutes?

Range: Static snapshot ↔ morning/evening modes ↔ continuous adaptation

User Agency Spectrum

How much agency does the user want in the triage flow vs. wanting the system to just get it right? This likely varies by email type, trust level, and tenure. We need the right default posture and how it should evolve.

3. Research Architecture

Pre-Phase: The Concept Gauntlet

Weeks 1–2

Goal: Go wide before going deep. Take 4 distinct experience visions, run them through rapid evaluation, and identify which 2–3 deserve the investment of full interactive prototype builds.

The Two-Lens Stimulus Package

Each concept is expressed through two complementary lenses. Together, they test the idea and the logic of each vision:

1. Value Prop Landing Page with Embedded Motion
The promise in plain language, plus animated sections showing surface dynamics. Headline, description, key screens with motion that hints at behavior. Tests "would I want this?" and "does it feel right?" in a single artifact. Built as polished single-page sites, deployed to Vercel, linkable from dScout.

2. Narrated Video Walkthrough
Screen-recorded walkthrough of a realistic morning email processing scenario with voiceover. Uses the Busy Parent persona throughout so participants compare concepts, not data. ~60–90 seconds each. Tests whether the logic and flow make sense — "could I do this every morning?"

Why Two Lenses

They deliberately test different things and may produce divergent signal for the same concept:

  • Landing page resonance ≠ walkthrough clarity: A concept might score high on "I want that" but low on "I understand how it works." That's a communication problem, not a concept problem — tells you where to invest in the prototype.
  • Walkthrough clarity ≠ landing page resonance: A concept might make perfect sense when narrated but lack a compelling value prop. That's a positioning problem, not a design problem.

These divergences tell you not just which concepts to advance but what to fix about them.

Evaluation

dScout Express mission. Participants see 2 concepts/day over 2 days, responding after each lens:

  • After landing page: "Would you want this? What excites you? What worries you? How does it feel?"
  • After walkthrough: "Does this make sense? Where did you get lost? Could you see yourself doing this every morning?"
  • After full package: "Compared to your real inbox, is this better? Would this replace your current email? What's the single biggest thing that would need to change?"

Gauntlet Output

  • 2–3 concepts advanced to full prototype builds
  • Design dimension priorities: which dimensions mattered most to participants
  • Concept refinement briefs: what worked, what needs to change per advancing concept

Track A — Intelligence Calibration (Shadow Inbox + WoZ)

Weeks 2–3

Goal: Calibrate Life Graph judgment quality through daily Wizard-of-Oz simulation against real participant email data. This is a calibration sprint (1–2 weeks), not an extended study.

The Shadow Inbox Method

Each participant maintains their real inbox as-is, but receives a daily simulated view of what the Home Surface would have shown them. This creates a direct comparison loop: did the system get it right?

5 internal dogfooders. Weekdays only.

Daily Cycle

1Morning input (participant, ~5 min): dScout diary — screenshots, voice narration of what's important, structured fields for top priorities and upcoming items.

2WoZ processing (operator, ~8–15 min/participant): Feed input into Claude prompt template that simulates Home Surface logic — prioritizing, categorizing, generating micro-briefs, separating incoming from upcoming. Output rendered into high-fidelity static mockup. Batch all participants after a morning cutoff.

3Shadow Inbox delivery (within 1–2 hours): Participant receives their personalized view while morning context is fresh.

4Afternoon reaction (participant, ~3–5 min): Evaluate the Shadow Inbox — priorities right? Anything missed? Miscategorized? Completion signal feel earned?

Treatment Variations

On select days, the treatment varies without informing participants (within-subjects):

DimensionVariations
AgencyAggressive (system handled low-priority, receipts only) vs. Conservative (everything surfaced with priority tiers)
CompletionProgress indicator vs. affirmative statement vs. priority countdown vs. no signal
DensityFull micro-briefs per item vs. category-level summaries only
TemporalMorning-optimized (upcoming-heavy) vs. catch-up (since-last-visit-heavy) vs. unified

Metrics

  • Priority accuracy (~80% target): system's top-3 vs. participant's self-reported top-3
  • Miss rate: how often the system fails to surface something important
  • Trust trajectory: does confidence increase, plateau, or erode?
  • Completion signal effectiveness: which variant produces the most confidence to stop checking?
  • Density preference: does preference correlate with volume or email type?
The Intelligence Readiness Gate
Track A isn't just tuning the prompt — it produces evidence about whether the intelligence layer is feasible in-house. Key outputs:
  • Priority accuracy trajectory over the 1–2 weeks (improving? plateauing? stuck?)
  • Failure mode breakdown (missing context? wrong categories? bad prioritization?) — tells you whether gaps are solvable with more tuning or are fundamental
  • Honest comparison: what a well-prompted frontier LLM can do today vs. what the product needs
  • Recommendation: build in-house vs. bring in vendor consultation

Recruitment criteria for the 5 (each participant is high-value — choose carefully):

  • Genuine email diversity (not 5 PMs with identical inboxes)
  • High daily volume (enough signal for the WoZ to work with)
  • Reliable — will actually screenshot and narrate every morning
  • Internal credibility — their eventual advocacy carries weight

This group rolls off after 1–2 weeks. Their purpose is to calibrate the intelligence and build internal conviction. WoZ learnings get baked into the prototypes the external panel evaluates.

Track B — Experience Design Exploration

Weeks 3–5

Goal: The 2–3 Gauntlet winners are built as full interactive prototypes. Each is a complete experience that takes deliberate positions across the full design space. These are research-owned, instrumented web apps — the "pre-TestFlight" version.

Relationship to the Gauntlet

The Gauntlet determines which concepts enter Track B and what to emphasize. A concept that scored high on value prop but felt too dense enters with a mandate to solve density. A concept that made sense in the walkthrough but lacked excitement enters with a mandate to sharpen the value prop. The Gauntlet doesn't just select — it refines.

Intelligence Is Pre-Calibrated

What we learned from the dogfood WoZ group in Track A gets baked into the prototypes. The external panel never does Shadow Inbox — they experience the refined product directly.

Prototype Delivery

Mobile-first web apps running in mobile Safari via URL. Phone-only with viewport lock for prototypes — no TestFlight, no App Store. Participants get a link. Gauntlet stimulus (landing pages + walkthrough videos) viewable on any device.

Testing Methods

Fresh dScout panel — external recruits with no product vision context:

  • dScout Express: Use prototype, then go to real inbox, narrate comparison. Multi-day diary entries build comparative signal.
  • Moderated labs (8–10 sessions): Think-aloud, scan pattern observation, cognitive framing probes, processing speed measurement.
  • Built-in analytics: Tap targets, scroll depth, time-on-surface, drill-down rate, triage completion time, return-to-surface frequency.

Convergence

WoZ learnings from Track A are baked into Track B prototypes before the external panel sees them. Cross-track insights emerge through the prototype testing itself: Do people tolerate less detail when intelligence is accurate? Does trust build faster in certain frames? Which completion signal works best when triage is actually reliable?

Quality gate: If Track A calibration isn't sufficient by Week 3, prototypes ship with curated data instead of WoZ-informed data. Track B is not blocked.

5. Concept Directions (4 Gauntlet Candidates)

ConceptMetaphorDescription
The Morning Brief Document you read Narrative-first. Reads like a personalized briefing. High density, low interaction demand. Incoming and upcoming woven together by relevance. Completion = reaching the end of the briefing.
The Living Canvas Landscape you survey Spatial, visual. Category tiles or zones that breathe by urgency. Priority communicated through visual weight and position. Lower per-item density, higher spatial information. Triage through manipulation. Completion = visual calm.
The Two-Stack Desk trays Maximum simplicity. Two buckets: incoming and upcoming. Satisfying "lift" interaction to move items between them. High density within stacks. Categories subordinate. Completion = incoming stack acknowledged.
The Chief of Staff Assistant reporting Maximum delegation. Three tiers: Handled / Needs You / FYI. System has already decided. User reviews and overrides. Low interaction demand, high trust required. Completion = "Needs You" cleared.

10. Week 5 Deliverables

Two distinct outputs for cross-functional leadership (research, design, eng, PM):

1. Experience Direction Deck
"Here are the 2 strongest directions and what each needs to become a product." Funnel narrowing backed by behavioral data, qualitative signal, and instrumented analytics from the prototypes.
2. Intelligence Readiness Assessment
"Can our team build this, or do we need vendor help?" Go/no-go on capability with accuracy trajectory, failure mode analysis, and a clear build-vs-vendor recommendation.

The prototype itself is a deliverable for eng — a working reference implementation with an analytics schema, not a Figma file to interpret.
