Track A Intelligence Calibration
Ongoing · Shadow Inbox + Method Toolkit
The big question: The Home Surface depends on AI that understands your email life — what's urgent, what's upcoming, what can wait. Before we can test the experience, we need to know if the intelligence behind it is good enough. This track is ongoing research with 5–7 dedicated dogfooders who fit our target audience — overwhelmed, high-volume email users.

How the Shadow Inbox Works

5–7 dogfooders from our target audience keep using their normal inbox. Each day, we simulate what the Home Surface would have shown them and ask: did we get it right? Think of it as a daily "report card" for the AI.

1
Morning: Participant screenshots their real inbox and tells us what's important to them today and what's coming up this week. (~5 min)
2
Mid-morning: We feed that information into an AI prompt that simulates what the Home Surface would show — prioritized emails, categories, micro-briefs, a "since you were last here" summary. (~8–15 min per person)
3
Before lunch: Participant receives their personalized "Shadow Inbox" — a realistic mockup of what this product would look like with their emails.
4
Afternoon: Participant rates how well we did. Were the priorities right? Did we miss anything important? Did the categories make sense? (~3–5 min)
Why this matters beyond tuning: This isn't just about getting the AI right. It produces a clear picture of where the intelligence stands, which capabilities are strong, and where we need to focus investment. We track accuracy over time — if it's improving, we know we're on the right path. If it plateaus, we know exactly which areas need a different approach.
?
Priority accuracy: Does the AI's "most important" match what the person actually cares about? Target: ~80%.
?
Failure patterns: When it's wrong, why? Missing context it can't see? Miscategorizing? Wrong urgency read?
?
Learning curve: Does accuracy get better each day as we refine the prompts, or does it plateau?
?
Where to focus investment: Which capabilities are strong? Which need a different approach or focused effort?
?
Presentation preferences: On some days we vary how much detail we show (full briefs vs. summaries) and how "done" is signaled — what do people prefer?

Convergence Bringing It Together Weeks 4–5
Tracks A and B are designed to run separately, then merge. Here's what that looks like: everything we learned about the AI's intelligence quality in Track A gets built into the experience prototypes from Track B. The result is a prototype that doesn't just look right — it's powered by intelligence we've already validated against real email data. The external panel in Weeks 4–5 experiences this integrated version.
This is also where the most interesting insights emerge — because the two tracks interact in ways you can't predict from either one alone:
Accuracy changes tolerance People may accept a denser, more information-heavy surface when they trust the AI is getting priorities right. If the intelligence is unreliable, the same density feels overwhelming.
Framing changes trust The same AI accuracy might feel trustworthy in a "Chief of Staff" frame (the system decided for you) but anxiety-inducing in a "Morning Brief" frame (did I miss something in the narrative?).
Completion depends on accuracy A "you're all caught up" signal only works if the system actually caught everything. The right completion model may be different at 70% accuracy vs. 90%.
?
Does the integrated experience hold up? An experience that worked with curated data may feel different with real AI-generated content.
?
What's the minimum intelligence bar? Below what accuracy threshold does the experience fall apart, regardless of how well-designed it is?
?
Which direction is strongest overall? With both experience design and intelligence quality factored in — what are we most confident shipping?
