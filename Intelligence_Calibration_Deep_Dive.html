Track A

Intelligence Calibration

Yahoo Mail Premium · Home Surface Research · Weeks 2–3

The Home Surface depends on AI that understands your email life — what's urgent, what's upcoming, what can wait. Before we can test the full experience, we need to know if the intelligence behind it is good enough. This track is an ongoing research effort with 5–7 dedicated dogfooders who fit our target audience — overwhelmed, high-volume email users whose inboxes have gotten away from them. Using their real inboxes, we calibrate the intelligence, identify where it breaks down, and build confidence in what works. Calibration quality also directly affects the Concept Gauntlet's Live Data Cohort, where participants port their own email data into each prototype and see intelligence output with all its current quirks and flaws — making every calibration improvement here doubly important.

The 7 Questions We Need to Answer
1 Personal importance vs. generic urgency

The permission slip due Friday matters more to a parent than the LinkedIn notification, even though both are "time-sensitive." Can the system distinguish what matters to this specific person from what's objectively urgent?

2 Connecting dots across messages

The contractor email Monday + payment reminder Wednesday + scheduling email today = "your kitchen renovation needs three things from you this week." Can it synthesize related threads, or does it treat every email as standalone?

3 The "since you were last here" summary

Too vague and people don't trust it. Too detailed and it's just another inbox. Can the intelligence find the right altitude — complete enough to feel safe, concise enough to feel like relief?

4 Improvement trajectory over time

If Day 1 is 50% accuracy and Day 10 is 75% with the curve still rising, we have a path. If Day 10 is 55%, we know exactly where to focus. The trajectory matters more than any single day's score — it tells us what's working, what needs rethinking, and how fast we're converging.

5 Does it feel like it knows you?

Two systems can be equally accurate but feel completely different. One feels like a trusted assistant who understands your life. The other feels like an algorithm. The difference is in tone, warmth, and small details — calling your kid's school by name vs. "a school-related email."

6 Graceful failure

The system will never be 100%. Does a missed priority feel catastrophic or minor? Does one bad call erode confidence in the whole surface? Confident and wrong is far worse than humble and slightly off. How does it handle its own uncertainty?

7 Quality across frequencies and volumes

Checking in after 2 hours with 4 new emails is a fundamentally different challenge than returning Monday morning to 60 weekend emails. The intelligence needs to scale both directions — concise when there's little, comprehensive when there's a lot — and the "since you were last here" framing needs to feel appropriate whether the gap is 90 minutes or 48 hours.

Track A

Method Toolkit

A flexible set of research methods — not a fixed script

Our approach

Rather than a single rigid daily plan, we use a toolkit of 6 methods and pick 2–3 per week based on what we're learning and where we're stuck. Some methods run throughout (Shadow Inbox as a pulse check). Others are situational (Stress Tests when a hard day naturally occurs, Real-Time Walkthroughs when we need to understand why something failed). This keeps us responsive to reality instead of locked into a plan that may not fit what we're finding. The toolkit's output also informs how we curate aspirational intelligence for the Gauntlet's Anonymized Sample Data Cohort — real but anonymized email data with output hand-tuned to represent the eventual quality we're striving toward.

1
Shadow Inbox
We create the "ideal" AI-generated version of what the Home Surface would show each participant, based on their real inbox that morning. They receive it within 1–2 hours and rate how well we did — were priorities right? Anything missed? Did the summary feel complete? This is the closest thing to testing the real product without having the real product.
Best for: Accuracy baseline, tracking improvement, testing the topline summary
When: Throughout — recurring pulse check
Effort: ~8–15 min/participant (operator) + ~8 min/day (participant)
2
Side-by-Side Model Comparison
Show the participant 2–3 different AI outputs for the same inbox snapshot. These might use different context graph versions, different prompt strategies, or different amounts of life context. The participant sees them together and tells us: which one got your priorities right? Which one feels like it actually knows you? Which one would you trust to show you every morning?
Best for: Choosing between competing AI approaches, testing warmth vs. accuracy tradeoffs
When: When we have hypotheses about prompt structure or context depth and need a tiebreaker
Effort: ~20 min/participant (operator generates 2–3 variants) + ~10 min (participant)
3
Display Variation Testing
Same intelligence output, presented in different ways. We vary the structure (grouped by category vs. ranked by priority vs. woven into a narrative), the length (one-liner per email vs. full micro-brief), and the tone (warm and conversational vs. clean and efficient). This separates whether the participant's reaction is about the intelligence being wrong or the display not working.
Best for: Isolating presentation quality from intelligence quality, finding the right density and tone
When: After intelligence accuracy stabilizes — so display is the variable, not a confound
Effort: ~15 min/participant (operator renders variants) + ~10 min (participant)
4
Real-Time Walkthrough & Annotation
A live session where we show the participant a Shadow Inbox output and walk through it together. They narrate their reactions in real time: "This is right... this is wrong... you don't know that my kid is allergic and this is actually from the allergist, not spam... this one you nailed, I wouldn't have thought to surface it." This is the richest signal — it surfaces invisible context and the why behind every failure.
Best for: Understanding why accuracy fails, surfacing context the AI can't see
When: When stuck on accuracy gaps — even 2–3 sessions can be more illuminating than a week of ratings
Effort: ~20–30 min/session (live, moderated)
5
Build-Your-Own Briefing
We give participants their raw inbox data and ask them to create the ideal Home Surface view. What would you prioritize? How would you group these? What would the summary say? This establishes the "gold standard" — what perfect actually looks like for this person — and reveals mental models. Do they think in categories, timelines, or priority tiers? Their ideal becomes the benchmark.
Best for: Establishing the target, revealing how people naturally organize their email life
When: Early (baseline before AI exposure) and again after — does their ideal shift?
Effort: ~15–20 min/participant (structured exercise, 1–2 times total)
6
Stress Test Scenarios
Deliberately throw hard days at the system. What happens with 60 emails after a long weekend? One genuinely urgent item buried in 30 routine ones? A day where the most important thing came from a verbal conversation the AI can't see? The system probably works fine on an average Tuesday — the question is what happens on hard days, when users need it most and where trust gets built or broken.
Best for: Finding the breaking point, understanding failure at the edges
When: Mid-to-late sprint, and opportunistically when a participant has a naturally unusual day
Effort: ~15 min/participant (curated scenario or flagged natural day)
Timeline — 1 to 2 Weeks

Week 1 (Pre-Sprint)

Build & stress-test prompt template. Recruit 5 dogfooders. Run Build-Your-Own Briefing as baseline exercise. Establish gold standards.

Days 1–3

Shadow Inbox daily to establish accuracy baseline. 1–2 Real-Time Walkthroughs to understand early failure patterns. Begin prompt iteration.

Days 4–7

Side-by-Side Comparisons to test competing approaches. Display Variations once intelligence stabilizes. Stress Tests if natural hard days arise. Iterate daily.

Days 8–10

Final Shadow Inbox runs for trajectory data. Stress Tests on remaining edge cases. Compile accuracy trajectory. Produce readiness assessment.

Intelligence Readiness Gate — What We Need to Know

This research produces clear evidence of where the intelligence stands and what it needs to reach product quality. The answer depends on the trajectory, not any single day's score.

Green: Trajectory is rising

Accuracy improving steadily. Failure modes are identifiable and fixable. Gaps are tuning problems, not fundamental. Clear path to product-quality intelligence.

Yellow: Plateau with known gaps

Accuracy decent but stuck. Specific failure modes clear (e.g., cross-message synthesis). We know exactly which capabilities need focused investment to close the gap.

Red: Plateau well below threshold

Accuracy stuck low. Failure modes are broad or fundamental. Requires a significant rethink of the intelligence approach — different architecture, more context sources, or different model strategy.
